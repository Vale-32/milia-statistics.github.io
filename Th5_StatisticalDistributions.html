Statistical Distributions: Continuous, Discrete, Properties and simulations

Abstract: A probability distribution is a mathematical function that provides the probabilities of the occurrence of various possible outcomes in an experiment. Statistical distributions play a crucial role in analyzing and interpreting data across various fields, providing insights into the patterns and characteristics of datasets. Two fundamental types of distributions are continuous and discrete distributions.

1. Discrete distributions

Discrete distributions deal with countable outcomes and are often associated with variables that take on distinct, separate values. Common examples of discrete distributions include the binomial distribution and the Poisson distribution. The binomial distribution, for instance, is used to model the number of successes in a fixed number of independent Bernoulli trials. Discrete distributions are characterized by probability mass functions, which assign probabilities to individual points in the dataset.

2. Continuous distributions

In contrast, continuous distributions deal with uncountable outcomes and are associated with variables that can take on any value within a given range. The normal distribution and the exponential distribution are examples of continuous distributions. The normal distribution, often referred to as the bell curve, is prevalent in various natural phenomena and is characterized by a probability density function. Unlike discrete distributions, continuous distributions assign probabilities to intervals rather than specific points.

3. Properties

Discrete distribution

  • Discrete Random Variables The random variable \(X\) takes on distinct, separate values.
  • Probability Mass Function (PMF) Describes the probability of each possible outcome. \(P(X = x)\) gives the probability of the random variable \(X\) taking the value \(x\).
  • Probability Space The sum of all probabilities for all possible outcomes equals 1, \(\sum {P(X = x)} = 1\).
  • Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF) \( F(x) = P(X \leq x) \) gives the probability that the random variable \(X\) is less than or equal to \(x\).
  • Expectation Often denoted as \(E(X)\), is a measure that represents the average or mean value of a random variable. The expectation of a random variable is calculated by taking the sum of the products of each possible value of the variable and its corresponding probability. For a discrete random variable \(X\) with probability mass function \(P(X)\), the expectation is given by: \( E(X) = \sum_{i}{x_i * P(X = x_i)} \)
  • Variance Measure of the dispersion or spread of a set of data. Variance provides valuable information about the degree of variability or spread in a dataset. A higher variance indicates that the data points are more dispersed from the mean, while a lower variance suggests that the data points are closer to the mean. Variance is a key component in the calculation of standard deviation, another important measure of the spread of data. For a sample of data, the sample variance \(s^2\) is given by: \(s^2 = \frac{1}{n-1} \sum_{i=1}^{n}{(X_i - \hat{X})^2} \), where \(n\) is the number of data points, \(X_i\) represents each individual data point and \(\hat{X}\) is the sample mean.

Continue distribution
  • Continuous Random Variables The random variable \(X\) can take any value within a range \( [a;b] \).
  • Probability Density Function (PDF) Describes the likelihood of a random variable taking on a particular value in a continuous range. \( P(a \leq X \leq b) = \int_a^b{f(x) dx}\), where \(f(x)\) is the PDF.
  • Probability Space The area under the entire PDF curve is equal to 1, \(\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}{f(x) dx} = 1 \)
  • Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF) \(F(x) = P(X \leq x)\) gives the probability that the random variable \(X\) is less than or equal to \(x\).
  • Expectation For a continuous random variable \(X\) with probability density function \(f(x)\), the expectation is calculated as an integral over the entire range of possible values: \(E(X) = \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}{x * f(x) dx}\)
  • Variance Population variance \(\sigma^2\) is calculated using a similar formula, but with \(n\) replaced by \(N\), where \(N\) is the total number of elements in the population, and \(\mu\) is the population mean: \(\sigma^2 = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N}{(X_i - \mu)^2} \)


4. Practical Applications

Real-World Example of Discrete Distribution
Consider a scenario where you are interested in modeling the number of defective items produced by a manufacturing process in a day. The random variable here is the count of defective items, and it takes on discrete values (0, 1, 2, 3, and so on). The probability mass function of a binomial distribution can be used to model this situation. The binomial distribution provides the probabilities of obtaining a specific number of successes (defective items) in a fixed number of independent trials (production runs).
For instance, let's say the probability of producing a defective item on any given trial is \(p=0.05\), and you are interested in the number of defective items in 10 trials. The binomial distribution can be used to calculate the probabilities of 0, 1, 2, ..., 10 defective items in a day.

Real-World Example of Continuous Distribution
Imagine you are studying the heights of individuals in a population. The random variable here is height, and it can take on any value within a range. In this case, a normal distribution (also known as a Gaussian distribution) can be a suitable model. The normal distribution is characterized by a bell-shaped curve and is commonly used to describe natural phenomena like human height.
Suppose the mean height in a population is 170 cm, and the standard deviation is 10 cm. You can use the probability density function of the normal distribution to calculate the probability of finding individuals with heights within certain ranges. For example, you can find the probability that a randomly selected individual has a height between 160 cm and 180 cm.

5. Challenges and Considerations

While discrete and continuous distributions offer powerful tools for statistical analysis, it is essential to consider the limitations and assumptions associated with each. Discrete distributions may oversimplify real-world scenarios where outcomes are not perfectly distinct, while continuous distributions may assume a level of precision that may not be realistic in certain contexts. Careful consideration of the underlying assumptions is crucial to avoid misinterpretation of results.

6. Conclusion

In conclusion, statistical distributions, whether discrete or continuous, form the foundation of statistical analysis. The choice between these distributions depends on the nature of the data and the characteristics of the variables being studied. Discrete distributions are suitable for countable outcomes, while continuous distributions are more appropriate for variables with a range of possible values. A thorough understanding of the characteristics, applications, and limitations of both types of distributions is essential for researchers and practitioners in making informed decisions in data analysis and modeling.
